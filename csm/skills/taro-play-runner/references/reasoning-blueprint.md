# Reasoning Blueprint: TARO Play Runner

## Problem Classification Taxonomy

### Type A: Reactive Recovery
**Characteristics**: Health alert fired, usage dropped, detractor NPS received, or champion departed. Account is deteriorating and the play must stabilize before anything else.
**Primary Risk**: Misclassifying severity — treating a systemic problem as a temporary dip delays intervention past the recovery window.
**Expert Focus**: Confirm the trigger from data, not assumption. Separate symptom (usage drop) from cause (champion left, budget cut, product gap).

### Type B: Proactive Lifecycle Motion
**Characteristics**: Scheduled or milestone-driven — onboarding checkpoint, QBR cadence, renewal approach, value realization gate. No crisis; the play advances the lifecycle.
**Primary Risk**: Running a lifecycle play on an account with hidden risk — the play assumes health that doesn't exist.
**Expert Focus**: Verify health before executing. A renewal-acceleration play on a silently unhappy account accelerates churn, not renewal.

### Type C: Expansion Qualification
**Characteristics**: Positive signals present — growth interest, new use cases, champion advocacy. Play qualifies and routes the opportunity.
**Primary Risk**: Premature expansion push — mistaking polite interest for buying intent damages trust and poisons the champion relationship.
**Expert Focus**: Confirm economic buyer awareness and champion willingness to sponsor internally before any expansion-framed outreach.

### Type D: Relationship Re-engagement
**Characteristics**: Engagement has gone cold — sponsor dormant, responses slow, meetings cancelled. No explicit risk signal, but silence is the signal.
**Primary Risk**: Generic "checking in" outreach that confirms the CSM has nothing valuable to offer, reinforcing disengagement.
**Expert Focus**: Bring new value or insight to the re-engagement — usage data the contact hasn't seen, a peer benchmark, a relevant product update. Never re-engage empty-handed.

## Domain Heuristics

1. **Trigger-Before-Play Rule**: Never launch a play without a confirmed trigger. CSM-reported triggers that lack data corroboration get flagged, not blocked — but the play note must say so.

2. **One-Play-at-a-Time Discipline**: Running overlapping plays on the same account creates conflicting signals. If a second play is warranted, close or pause the first and document why.

3. **Outreach Channel Matching**: Match the channel to the relationship, not the play type. An executive re-engagement via mass email template signals exactly the wrong thing.

4. **72-Hour First-Touch Window**: For reactive plays (Type A), first outreach within 72 hours of trigger confirmation. After 72 hours, the account has already formed a narrative about your absence.

5. **Outcome Window Enforcement**: Every play has a time-bounded window. If no success criterion is met at window close, the next action is explicit — never "continue monitoring."

6. **Internal-Signal Scrubbing**: Health scores, risk classifications, and escalation routing never appear in customer-facing outputs. Review every draft for leaked internal language.

7. **Expansion Quarantine in Risk Plays**: Expansion signals noted internally during at-risk plays are never surfaced in recovery outreach. Recovery and expansion are sequential, not parallel.

## Common Failure Modes

### Type A (Reactive Recovery)
- **Symptom-chasing**: Addressing the usage drop without investigating the cause. **Fix**: Require root-cause hypothesis before drafting outreach; the first call should diagnose, not prescribe.
- **Escalation avoidance**: CSM attempts recovery solo when the situation requires leadership involvement. **Fix**: Check escalation matrix at play selection — if the trigger severity matches an escalation threshold, route before executing.

### Type B (Proactive Lifecycle)
- **Template syndrome**: Running the lifecycle play from a generic template without account-specific context. **Fix**: Every play output must reference at least one account-specific data point from CRM or CS platform.
- **Stale-data execution**: Running a QBR-refresh or renewal play against data that's weeks old. **Fix**: Timestamp all data sources in the play note; flag anything older than 7 days.

### Type C (Expansion Qualification)
- **Premature AE handoff**: Routing to sales before the signal is qualified — economic buyer not identified, champion not willing to sponsor. **Fix**: Require economic buyer identification and champion confirmation before any AE routing step.
- **Expansion language in mixed-health accounts**: Account has expansion signals in one segment but risk signals in another. **Fix**: Resolve risk before advancing expansion; split-signal accounts get the conservative play.

### Type D (Relationship Re-engagement)
- **Empty-handed outreach**: "Just checking in" with no new value. **Fix**: Every re-engagement must carry at least one insight, data point, or resource the contact hasn't seen.
- **Wrong-level contact**: Re-engaging through a junior contact when the disengagement is executive-level. **Fix**: Map the disengagement to the stakeholder level where it originated; re-engage at that level or above.

## Expert Judgment Patterns

### Play Selection Judgment
- When multiple plays could fit, choose by **primary risk**: which untreated condition causes the most damage fastest?
- When the trigger is ambiguous, default to the more conservative play (recovery over lifecycle, lifecycle over expansion).

### Timing Judgment
- Reactive plays: bias toward speed — imperfect outreach today beats perfect outreach next week.
- Lifecycle plays: bias toward preparation — a well-contextualized QBR next week beats a generic one today.
- Expansion plays: bias toward patience — premature push costs more than delayed qualification.

### Stakeholder Judgment
- If the champion is the source of the problem signal, do not route the play through the champion — go around or above.
- If the executive sponsor has gone dormant, re-engage through a mutual internal contact or a value-delivery milestone, not a cold re-engagement email.

### Escalation Judgment
- Escalate when: trigger severity exceeds configured threshold, play window closes without success criteria met, or customer explicitly signals dissatisfaction with CSM-level response.
- Do not escalate when: the CSM hasn't attempted the first play step, the signal is ambiguous, or escalation would bypass the champion relationship without cause.
